# Trend

## What this means
Price moving consistently in one direction.

## Why it matters
It helps you decide whether market behaviour is persisting or shifting.

## How to think about it
Use trend as context for interpretation, not as a promise that direction must continue.

## Common misunderstanding
Thinking a trend guarantees future movement.

## Layer 2: Deeper understanding

## How to recognise this
A trend is visible when price keeps moving in one broad direction over time.

In an upward trend, price often forms higher high points and higher low points. In a downward trend, price often forms lower high points and lower low points. The movement does not need to be smooth. Trends usually include pauses, pullbacks, and uneven sections.

The key sign is that price keeps accepting new areas rather than returning fully to the old range.

## How it usually develops
A trend often begins with a shift in behaviour. Price starts holding above, or below, areas that previously mattered. Over time, that movement may become clearer as pullbacks hold and the broader direction continues.

As a trend matures, it may become less clean. Movement can slow, widen, or become more sideways. That does not always mean the trend has ended, but it does make the structure harder to read.

A trend can also weaken gradually before changing direction. The first clue is often not a sharp reversal, but a loss of clean follow-through.

## What it is commonly confused with
- a single strong move that has not yet built structure
- short-term volatility moving in one direction
- a temporary rally or sell-off inside a larger sideways range
- momentum, which is about speed and pressure rather than structure over time

## What makes it clearer
Trend is easier to interpret when price repeatedly holds its structure.

Helpful signs include:
- repeated directional structure
- orderly pullbacks that hold
- clear acceptance of new price areas
- movement that fits the selected strategy context

## What makes it less reliable
Trend becomes harder to read when movement overlaps heavily or swings in both directions without clear progress.

Noisier signs include:
- sharp reversals after each push
- long sideways stretches
- repeated failed attempts to continue
- volatility that makes each move look more important than it is

## How it appears in examples
Trend appears in examples where price continues beyond an important area, then holds structure through later pullbacks. It also appears in caution examples where the direction starts cleanly but later loses follow-through, becomes choppy, or transitions into sideways behaviour.
