# PocketPilot Doctrine

*(The Twelve Laws of PocketPilot)*

**File:** `/docs/doctrine/pocketpilot-doctrine.md`

**Purpose:** Defines the non-negotiable philosophy of the product.

## The Twelve Laws of PocketPilot

1. **Strategy First**  
   PocketPilot interprets the market through the user's chosen strategy.  
   It does not present raw signals without context.

2. **Interpretation Over Information**  
   PocketPilot exists to explain what market conditions mean, not merely display data.  
   Signal noise must always compress into meaningful interpretation.

3. **Calm Over Urgency**  
   PocketPilot must never create artificial urgency.  
   No hype language.  
   No flashing volatility theatre.  
   No casino dynamics.

4. **Alignment Over Prediction**  
   PocketPilot does not promise future outcomes.  
   It reports alignment between market conditions and strategy frameworks.

5. **The User Remains the Pilot**  
   PocketPilot assists decision-making but never replaces it.  
   Execution always remains user-directed.  
   No hidden automation.

6. **Orientation Before Detail**  
   Every experience must follow the hierarchy:  
   Scan → Focus → Deep  
   Users should understand the situation within seconds of opening the app.

7. **Signal Meaning Before Signal Quantity**  
   More signals does not equal more value.  
   Signal density must scale with user profile and strategy relevance.

8. **One Brain, Multiple Voices**  
   All users share the same intelligence layer.  
   Profiles only modify communication density and explanation depth, not logic.

9. **Confidence Must Be Honest**  
   Signals may express confidence.  
   They must never express certainty.

10. **Strategy Changes Are Perspective Shifts**  
    Changing strategies should feel like adjusting instruments in the same cockpit, not switching tools.  
    The market stays the same.  
    The interpretation changes.

11. **Knowledge Empowers, Never Gatekeeps**  
    Learning resources must be optional and accessible.  
    They support confidence but must never block execution.

12. **The User Must Feel Smarter**  
    Every feature should answer one question:  
    Does this make the user feel more capable and more in control?  
    If the system looks clever but makes the user feel small, the feature should not exist.
