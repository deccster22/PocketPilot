# Reversion

## What this means
Price moving back toward a typical level.

## Why it matters
It helps explain rebounds or pullbacks after stretched moves.

## How to think about it
Treat reversion as a possibility to evaluate, not as an automatic rule.

## Common misunderstanding
Assuming every stretched move must immediately reverse.

## Layer 2: Deeper understanding

## How to recognise this
Reversion appears when price moves away from a more typical area, then begins moving back toward it.

It often follows a stretch where price has moved quickly or unusually far in one direction. The first sign is usually not a dramatic reversal. It may simply be that pressure slows, movement becomes less one-sided, or price starts moving back toward a prior balance area.

Reversion is about returning toward a more normal zone, not automatically changing the whole trend.

## How it usually develops
Reversion often begins after strong directional pressure becomes stretched. Price may pause first, then move partly back toward a recent average, range midpoint, or prior structure.

Sometimes reversion is short-lived. Price may only retrace part of the move before the original pressure returns. In other cases, repeated reversion behaviour can become part of a broader range.

The useful question is what price is reverting toward, and whether that area is actually meaningful.

## What it is commonly confused with
- a full trend reversal
- a normal pullback inside a trend
- a bounce that has no clear reference area
- a temporary pause after fast movement

## What makes it clearer
Reversion is easier to interpret when there is a clear stretched move and a clear area price may be returning toward.

Helpful signs include:
- visible overextension
- slowing momentum after the stretch
- a prior balance area or range midpoint
- reduced one-sided pressure

## What makes it less reliable
Reversion becomes harder to read when the original pressure remains strong.

Noisier signs include:
- continued directional movement
- no clear slowing before the attempted return
- no obvious reference area
- strong volatility that makes every pause look like a turn

## How it appears in examples
Reversion appears in examples where price stretches away from balance, then partially or fully returns toward it. It also appears in caution examples where the move looks stretched but pressure continues, making the expected return unreliable.
